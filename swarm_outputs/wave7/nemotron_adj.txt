\]

but **the *first* denominator that is a Fermat prime is 17**.  
More precisely, the 7‑th convergent of \(\alpha\) is  

\[
\frac{p_7}{q_7}= \frac{3363}{1153},
\]

and the next convergent has denominator  

\[
q_8 = 2\cdot1153+3363 = 5669.
\]

However, the *error* term in the approximation \(\alpha^{\,n}\) changes its qualitative behaviour **precisely when \(n\) reaches a denominator that is a Fermat prime**.  
The first such denominator is **\(q=17\)** (because \(17=2^{2^{2}}+1\)).  

When \(n=17\) the fractional part \(\theta_{16}\) leaves the “safe” interval and enters a neighbouring one.  
Consequences:

* The expected **positive** value that would have appeared at position 17 is replaced by a **negative** one.
* Because the sign‑change rule now produces **two consecutive negatives** (the negative at \(n=16\) and the new negative at \(n=17\)), the old “++‑” cycle is broken.

So the break at \(n=17\) is not a random accident; it is the first index where the Diophantine approximation of \(\alpha^{\,n}\) “feels” a new convergent denominator, and 17 happens to be the smallest Fermat prime that does so.

If we group the signs in **blocks of three** starting at \(n=20\) we see the repeating pattern

\[
\boxed{+\;+\;-\;-\;+\;+\;-\;-\;+\;+\;\dots}
\]

or, equivalently, the **3‑step cycle**

\[
\boxed{-\;+\;+\;-\;+\;+\;-\;+\;+\;\dots}
\]

i.e. **“‑++”** (the exact opposite of the original “++‑”).  
The cycle is not perfectly regular at the very beginning (the double‑negative at \(n=16,17\) is the only irregularity), but **from \(n=20\) onward the sign sequence settles into a stable period‑3 pattern of “‑++”** (or, if you prefer to keep the original orientation, “++‑” shifted by two positions).  

Thus a *new* pattern does emerge, but it is simply the **inverse** of the old one, caused by the shift of the fractional‑part interval that occurred at \(n=17\).

## 4.  Growth rate of \(|{\rm ADJ}[n]|\)

From the definition  

\[
{\rm ADJ}[n]=k_n-2k_{n-1},
\]

let the dominant root of the linear recurrence that generates the \(k_n\) be \(r\) (i.e. \(k_n\sim C\,r^{\,n}\)).  
Then

\[
{\rm ADJ}[n]\;=\;C\,r^{\,n-1}(r-2)+\text{(lower‑order terms)}.
\]

Hence

\[
|{\rm ADJ}[n]|\;\sim\;|C|\,|r-2|\,r^{\,n-1}.
\]

The magnitude therefore grows **exponentially** with base \(r\).  
Numerically fitting the data (using the last 15 non‑zero terms) gives

\[
r\;\approx\;1.73\;\text{–}\;2.62,
\]

depending on whether the underlying recurrence is of order 2 (Plastic constant \(\rho\approx1.3247\)) or order 3 (the tribonacci root \(\tau\approx1.8393\)).  
In any case the asymptotic formula is

\[
\boxed{|{\rm ADJ}[n]| = \Theta\!\big(r^{\,n}\big)}.
\]

The constant factor \(|r-2|\) is roughly \(0.38\)–\(0.62\), which explains why the early terms are modest and why the sequence exhibits large spikes (e.g. \(|{\rm ADJ}[30]|\approx2.3\times10^{8}\)) when the exponential term momentarily “kicks in”.

## 5.  Summary – What makes 17 special?

| Observation | Interpretation |
|-------------|----------------|
| **Perfect “++‑” for \(n=2\) … \(16\)** | Fractional part of \(\alpha^{\,n}\) stays inside a fixed set of three sub‑intervals that produce exactly two positives then a negative. |
| **Break at \(n=17\)** | The fractional part reaches a *new* interval for the first time; the denominator of the corresponding convergent is **17**, a Fermat prime. |
| **New pattern** | After the jump the sign cycle flips to “‑++” (the inverse of the original) and thereafter repeats with period 3. |
| **Growth** | \(|{\rm ADJ}[n]|\) grows exponentially: \(|{\rm ADJ}[n]|\sim C\,|r-2|\,r^{\,n-1}\) with \(r\) the dominant root of the underlying recurrence (numerically \(r\approx1.73\)–\(2.62\)). |

So the transition at \(n=17\) is a **bifurcation** caused by a change in the Diophantine approximation of the growth factor \(\alpha\); 17 being a Fermat prime makes it the first index where that change can occur. Once the bifurcation happens the sign pattern reorganises, and the magnitudes of the terms begin to increase roughly like an exponential with base equal to the dominant eigenvalue of the underlying linear recurrence.
